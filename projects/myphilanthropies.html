The Background

September 2019-December 2019

MyPhilanthropies is a non-profit organization in Irvine, California that promotes connectivity between project leaders and a network of volunteers. As the scope grew, the company faced a problem with storing and matching their volunteers with opportunities that fit their skillset.

They decided to partner up with students from UCI to create a brand-new system that supported their organizational needs. I joined the team as a front-end developer.

The Problem

Programs and events are continuously being added, removed, or changed.

Volunteers required up-to-date information to properly help at events. To stay efficient with limited human resource, the company needed a way to reach out to many people with minimal work.

Excel sheets took too long to fill out.

As an upcoming nonprofit company, they only had one person keeping track of all volunteer information. There were columns for volunteer names, age, skillset, and contact information. This became increasingly difficult to maintain as they gained more volunteers.

Volunteers had to be manually called or e-mailed for every event, which often led to ghosting.

The current system had the company call or email each individual whenever an event arose. This led them to miss a few volunteers that had the required skills, and volunteers often didn't check for new events if they missed a call or email.

The Research

We created user stories to serve as our requirements for the system. In order to get a better sense of our stakeholders, we created personas and scenarios based on our user stories.

Then, we started storyboarding how users would interact with our web application.

Before we started programming anything, we began to design UML class diagrams and sequence diagrams to determine the different parts of the website we needed to create and their functions.

The Features

Previous Next

Sign Up and Log In

We created a user database that allows for our clients to keep all information about the volunteers in one place. They no longer had to manually input things into an excel sheet such as name, age, and contact information. As soon as volunteers signed up, that data would be stored and be available for the admins to view at any time.

The more robust sign up process gave insight on what kind of volunteer they are dealing with and a general idea of their schedules. For example, unemployed volunteers could dedicate more time than full-time students. This also added a section which gave admins knowledge on the best way to communicate with volunteers.

Event Home Page

In order to make viewing event information easier, we created a list of events in the form of cards. Each card contained the event name, the date, the location, icons for skills needed, and a search bar to make filtering easier. We also included a navigation sidebar. Home would take users back to this page, inbox would be a in-app messaging system, events would lead users to events they've saved or signed up to, and settings would allows users to update their information.

The Lessons Learned

The Technology

This project had a difficult learning curve. This was the first time I ever worked with HTML/CSS/JavaScript. I spent countless of hours—days even—just to learn how ajax requests worked using jQuery for the sign up system. It was especially difficult learning about authentication keys and security measure we needed to secure users' personal information.

Halfway through the project, my team members and I realized that working with frameworks such as React or Angular would have made web development so much easier with their various libraries that supported the functionalities we needed to build. Coding in vanilla, however, was a painful but essential learning experience.

Nontechnical Tech Talk

Our clients are well-versed in fine arts. They are actors, singers, business leaders—not programmers. They had a vision for the "what" but not the "how". The ability to guide and explain design decisions became necessary to ensure customer happiness throughout the project. Meetings with our sponsors used a different dictionary than presenting the project to our classmates.

Sprinting CAN Be Fun

The quarter system is unforgiving. Even more so when working with a project of this size with real clients. It became important that every single person had a role and was aware of what others were doing so no two people overlapped. Communication, a skill that many members in group work struggle with, played a vital role in keeping things on track.

Sprint backlogs and reports became second-nature to us. Slack messages were continously being exchanged 24/7 as we developped and bug fixed on the same day. Pushing and pulling from GitHub repositories, and solving merge conflicts, became as easy pressing CTRL + S in code editors.
